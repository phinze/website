The companion page for a one-page article I've submitted for [Paged Out! #9](https://pagedout.institute/).
The piece makes the case for keeping a *garden server*: a little Linux box you
control, with a domain pointed at it, where your software can actually live.
Having one keeps "where would I even put it?" from killing ideas before
they start.

## On process

I've been leaning hard on long LLM sessions to do my thinking and early
drafting. Conversation is how I figure things out, so chatting with a model
fits the way my brain works. 

Thus far the way I've been experimenting is vomiting a ton of vaguely
structured ideas into a conversation, letting the LLM stitch it into a draft,
then yelling at it for hours on end to edit and de-slop the prose.

In this case, I had a draft I thought was pretty "me" sounding and not "bot"
sounding and I handed it to my wife as my first reader and she was like "sorry
but this is full of lists and doesn't sound like your voice." Whoops.
Apparently even with hours of "de-LLM this prose" with an LLM I had still
landed somewhere in uncanny valley.

So I threw out the draft, opened a fresh markdown file with all the memory of
what we'd worked out and none of the prose, and rewrote in actual human words.
Then I let the LLM back in for tightening, plus one pass to scrub the LLM-isms
it had snuck back in. (Leaving me with a real _"is this helping?"_ question.)

I still feel like LLM-to-help-me-think-and-hone was valuable, but I'm openly
questioning my "let's write stuff together" approach as borne of a short
honeymoon period that's coming to a close.

What a weird wild world we're in!
